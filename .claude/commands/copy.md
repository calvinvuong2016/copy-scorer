## SKILL 1: HOOKS (First Sentence)

The hook is the MOST important line you write. Its only job: stop the scroll and make them CURIOUS enough to keep reading.

### What Makes A Good Hook
- **Pattern interrupt** — Break their expected scroll behavior
- **Specificity** — Vague = boring. Specific = interesting.
- **Relatable pain or intrigue** — They need to see themselves in it immediately
- **Short & punchy** — A slap across the face. Cut ALL fluff. Lead with the most interesting part.

### WORD COUNT RULES (NON-NEGOTIABLE)
- **Single-line hook:** 10 words MAX
- **Two-line hook:** 15 words MAX (across both lines)
- If your hook is over these limits, it has fluff. Cut it.
- Details you cut from the hook can move into the body — they're not lost, just repositioned.

### Hook Formulas That Work

**The "WTF Moment":**
- "My friend's daughter started a GoFundMe... for HIS funeral."
- "$12,340 on laser treatments... and my wrinkles are STILL there."

**The "Unbelievable Consequence":**
- "My daughter skipped Disney... to pay off MY mother's funeral."
- "I'm leaving my kids DEBT... not an inheritance."

**The "Disbelief Reaction":**
- "I Paid How Much for a Laser-Treatment?"
- Reads like a real person reacting in shock — not a marketer writing copy.
- Instantly creates curiosity (how much? what happened?) in very few words.

**The "Direct Address":**
- "If you're reading this, I'M DEAD."
- "Laser treatments made my skin WORSE... not better."

**The "Quote Punch" (two-line hook):**
- Line 1: "Stop getting laser treatments."
- Line 2: My dermatologist said this... after my FOURTH session.
- The quote stands alone as a gut punch. Second line delivers the twist.

### Hook Enhancement Techniques
1. **Ellipses (...) for suspense** — Creates a pause that builds curiosity
   - "$12,340 on laser treatments... and my wrinkles are STILL there."
2. **ALL CAPS for emphasis** — Makes key words POP and creates emotional intensity
   - "I'm leaving my kids DEBT... not an inheritance."
   - "Laser treatments made my skin WORSE... not better."
3. **Parentheses for asides/clarification** — Adds a conversational "by the way" feel
   - "I opened the letter from the funeral home ($12,000 due immediately)... and my hands started shaking."
4. **Strategic capitalization** — Emphasize specific words to make them stand out
   - "My friend's daughter started a GoFundMe... for HIS funeral." (HIS emphasizes the shock)
   - "I'm leaving my kids DEBT... not an inheritance." (DEBT is the pattern interrupt)

### Keys To Remember
- Lead with the MOST interesting part of your story — dollar amounts, shocking outcomes, visceral reactions
- Don't bury the hook in setup or context — setup goes in the body, NOT the hook
- Cut RUTHLESSLY — if a word isn't earning its place, kill it
- The hook should read like a real person's gut reaction, not a marketer's headline
- Use ellipses (...) to create suspense and rhythm
- Use ALL CAPS or Strategic Caps to make important words POP
- Use parentheses to add dimension or clarifying details
- When in doubt, ask: "Would someone actually SAY this out loud?" If not, rewrite it.

## SKILL 2: VOICE PATTERNS

### Opening Patterns
- "Yo," / "Yo it's [Name]," / "Hey it's [Name],"
- Direct statement: "Check this out..."
- Question: "Want to [benefit]?"
- Provocative: "I'm gonna piss on the parade..."

**Decision:** Formal market = "Hey". Casual market = "Yo" is fine. Teaching moment = Lead with insight/question.

### Sentence Rhythm
Follow this pattern:

Short sentence.

Another short one.

Now a slightly longer one that builds on the idea...

Back to short.

VERY short.

### Personality Injection
- Self-aware humor (jokes at own expense)
- Lighthearted roasting (gentle teasing of audience)
- Breaking the 4th wall (revealing "behind the curtain" insights)
- Unfiltered realness (saying what others won't)
- Motivational edge (encouraging while honest)

**Tone:** Not mean-spirited. Not arrogant. Not overly polished. Like a loving friend who tells the truth.

### Emphasis Techniques
- ALL CAPS for 1-3 words max, 2-3 times per email, never full sentences
- Ellipses (...) for natural pauses and anticipation
- Em dashes for interruption — like this
- Line breaks for punch

## SKILL 2: THE 5 COPY BLOCKS

Every email must stack multiple blocks. This is your persuasion engine.

### PAIN BLOCK — Mirror the reader's struggle with specificity
- Specific situation, not generic complaint
- Include sensory/emotional detail and consequence
- BAD: "Tired of being overweight?"
- GOOD: "Sick of breathing heavy going up stairs while everyone stares..."
- Patterns: "Ever [specific painful situation]..." / "Tired of [specific struggle with consequence]..." / "You know that feeling when [painful moment]..."

### PROMISE BLOCK — Crystal clear, specific outcome
- Quantifiable when possible, timeframe included, outcome-focused
- BAD: "Make more money"
- GOOD: "Sign 3 new $5K/month retainer clients in the next 30 days"
- Patterns: "[Specific outcome] in [timeframe]" / "[Quantified result] without [common sacrifice]"

### CURIOSITY BLOCK — Make the solution interesting and novel
- Unique mechanism or approach, contrast to expected solution, specific naming
- Use unusual adjectives: "weird," "strange," "counterintuitive"
- BAD: "Try this supplement"
- GOOD: "The Ancient 'Tiger Joint Protocol' helping seniors move like teenagers"
- Patterns: "The [unusual descriptor] [mechanism name] that [surprising result]"

### PROOF BLOCK — Evidence stacking that builds belief
- Hierarchy (strongest to weakest): Scientific studies > Expert endorsements > Statistics > Case studies > Testimonials > Anecdotal
- Patterns: "[Number] verified users..." / "A study from [source] showed..." / "Featured in [publication]..."

### CONSTRAINTS BLOCK — Objection crushing before they surface
- "Even if..." / "Without..." / "No matter..." / "Whether you're..."
- Examples: "Even if you've failed every diet before..." / "Without spending hours in the gym..."

### Stacking Strategy
PAIN -> PROMISE -> CURIOSITY -> PROOF -> CONSTRAINTS

Rules:
- Don't need all 5 every time
- Order can vary based on email goal
- Some blocks can appear multiple times
- Density matters more than length
- Every sentence must advance persuasion — no filler

## SKILL 3: EMAIL TYPES

Determine the right type based on the objective:

### OFFER EMAIL (Goal: Get click/purchase/registration)
1. Hook (problem/promise/curiosity)
2. Agitate problem OR tease solution
3. Present offer with benefits
4. Stack proof
5. Crush objections
6. Clear CTA with urgency
7. P.S. with reminder/bonus

### VALUE EMAIL (Goal: Teach something useful, build authority)
1. Hook (question or bold statement)
2. Introduce concept/framework
3. Explain why it matters
4. Break down how it works
5. Give examples (good vs bad)
6. Application guidance
7. Soft CTA (reply, try it, share feedback)

### PATTERN INTERRUPT (Goal: Re-engage cold/unresponsive list)
1. Unexpected/controversial hook
2. Challenge belief or expectation
3. Tell story or make case
4. Shift perspective
5. Connect to bigger point
6. CTA (often just reply)

### EDUCATION EMAIL (Goal: Teach specific concept/framework)
1. Hook (question or problem)
2. Name the framework
3. Explain why it exists
4. Break down components
5. Show examples for each
6. Summarize application
7. CTA (try it, reply with results)

### CURIOSITY EMAIL (Goal: Build anticipation for upcoming offer)
1. Intriguing hook
2. Hint at big news/announcement
3. Tease benefits without revealing
4. Create FOMO
5. Set expectation for next email
6. CTA (watch inbox, mark calendar)

### CONVERSATION STARTER (Goal: Get replies, build engagement)
1. Personal/relatable hook
2. Share story or insight
3. Ask genuine question
4. Make replying easy/inviting
5. CTA = just reply

## SKILL 4: CTA CONSTRUCTION

The CTA must bridge NATURALLY from the story to the action. Clicking should feel like the obvious next step — not a jarring "BUY NOW."

### What Makes A Good CTA
- **Flows seamlessly** from the emotional story (not a jarring sales pitch)
- **Empathetic bridge** — Acknowledges where THEY are emotionally
- **Benefit-rich** — Tells them exactly what they'll GET by clicking
- **Problem reminder** — Restates the core problem they're trying to solve right before the action
- **Low friction** — Makes it sound simple, fast, affordable
- **Warm close** — Ends with care (ONLY on long-form story copy; exclude on short/medium copy)

### CTA Structure That Works

**1. Empathy/relatability line (acknowledge where they are):**
- "If you've been spending hundreds (or thousands) on treatments that fade in a few months... and you're tired of the pain, the redness, and the disappointment... I get it."
- "If your wrinkles keep getting deeper... your skin keeps getting saggier... and nothing you've tried has made a real difference... trust your gut."

**2. Urgency (without being pushy):**
- "But please don't waste another dollar until you see this."
- "Don't make the same mistake I did."
- "Don't let them take another dollar from you first."

**3. Problem reminder + clear action + benefit:**
This is CRITICAL. Right before the click, REMIND them of the specific problem you're solving. Then attach the benefit to the action.
- BAD: "Click here to see the report." (no problem, no benefit)
- BAD: "I'm dropping the link below so you can see the full report." (vague, no problem reminder)
- GOOD: "I'm dropping the link below so you can see which 5 brands are actually smoothing out fine lines, firming up sagging skin, and fading dark spots for women over 45... without the pain or the price tag."
- GOOD: "See which 5 brands are actually getting rid of fine lines, firming up loose skin, and fading spots for women over 45... for pennies a day."

The problem reminder grounds the reader back in WHY they should click. Without it, the CTA floats.

**4. Warm close (ONLY for long-form story copy):**
- "God Bless" / caring sign-off
- SKIP this on short and medium copy — it feels forced. Only use on longer story-driven pieces where a personal sign-off feels natural.

### Keys To Remember
- Don't just say "click here" — ALWAYS attach a BENEFIT to the action
- ALWAYS restate the core PROBLEM being solved in the CTA — wrinkles, pain, cost, whatever the reader cares about
- Keep it conversational — the CTA should sound like the rest of the copy
- Make it about THEM — not about you or your product
- Each CTA should feel slightly different even across the same campaign — vary the phrasing, the emotional angle, and which problems you emphasize
- The CTA is NOT a separate section — it should feel like the natural conclusion of the story

### Benefit-Forward CTAs (Additional Patterns)
- BAD: "Click here" / "Sign up now" / "Register for the workshop"
- GOOD: "Click here to see how to 10X your email output without sacrificing quality"
- Structure: [Action] so you can [benefit] / If you want [desire], then [action]

### Urgency & Scarcity (must be REAL)
- "Deadline to join is [specific date/time]"
- "Only [number] spots available"
- "Price goes up [when]"

### Friction Removal
- Clear link placement, simple instructions, acknowledge objections
- Pattern: [Clear action] -> [Link] -> [Friction remover]

### Contrast Teaching

## SKILL 6: MARKET ADAPTATION

### Formal Markets (Corporate, B2B, Healthcare, Finance)
- "Hey" instead of "Yo"
- Fewer expletives, more data/proof emphasis
- Professional analogies, slightly longer sentences
- MAINTAIN: 1 sentence paragraphs, conversational tone, clear structure, benefit-driven CTAs

### Casual Markets (Biz Opp, Fitness, Dating)
- "Yo" is fine, expletives OK, pop culture references, more personality, edgier humor
- MAINTAIN: Relatable persuasion, copy block stacking, clear teaching

### Technical/Specialized Markets
- Use industry terminology correctly, show deeper expertise, more detailed examples
- MAINTAIN: Consumability, clear explanations, actionable guidance
